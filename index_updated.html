Distortion-Aware 3D Reconstruction from 360° Media

Reconstructing real-world scenes from equirectangular and fisheye imagery using Gaussian-based scene representations, with machine-learning depth priors to improve geometric accuracy where wide-angle cameras distort the world most.

The Problem

Wide-angle cameras see more, but lie about scale

A 360° or fisheye camera captures an entire scene in one shot — but to store that capture as a flat image, the sphere has to be unwrapped. That unwrapping is not uniform: it stretches some regions and compresses others.

In an equirectangular projection (the flat "map of the world" format used for 360° images), pixels near the top and bottom of the image cover far less real-world area than pixels near the middle. A fisheye lens has a related but distinct distortion, concentrated toward the edges of the frame. Most 3D reconstruction methods were built and tuned for ordinary, undistorted camera images — applying them directly to 360° or fisheye footage means treating unevenly-stretched pixels as if they were all equal.

01 — Geometry

Uneven pixel density

The same patch of real-world surface is represented by very different numbers of pixels depending on where it falls in the projection.

02 — Training

Biased optimization

If left uncorrected, reconstruction methods spend disproportionate effort on oversampled regions and underfit the rest.

03 — Initialization

Weak starting geometry

Reconstruction quality depends heavily on a good initial guess of scene depth — and depth estimators are rarely trained on distorted, wide-angle images.

The Approach

A reconstruction pipeline built for 360° geometry, not against it

The thesis builds on 3DGUT, a recent Gaussian-splatting method that already supports non-standard camera models, and extends it with corrections specific to the distortion of wide-angle and 360° capture.

01

Distortion-aware weighting

Photometric training signal is reweighted according to the true solid angle each pixel represents — so oversampled regions (like the poles of an equirectangular image) stop dominating the optimization.

02

Depth-guided initialization

Monocular depth estimates, adapted for 360° input via cubemap decomposition, give the reconstruction a geometrically informed starting point rather than relying on sparse structure-from-motion points alone.

03

Latitude-stratified evaluation

Quality is measured separately across polar, mid-latitude, and equatorial regions — because a single averaged score can hide exactly the distortion-related errors this work is trying to fix.

04

Controlled, multi-projection comparison

The same physical scenes are reconstructed under pinhole, fisheye, and equirectangular capture, on one unified framework — making it possible to compare projections fairly rather than across different methods and datasets.

Current Status

Early-stage — methods implemented, results in progress

Active development — quantitative results withheld pending validation

The core pipeline — distortion-aware training, multi-projection support, and the latitude-stratified evaluation framework — is implemented and running. Several ablation experiments are complete; others, including the depth-guided initialization study, are in progress.

This page intentionally does not include numerical results. Findings are still being validated for statistical reliability before being reported, and will be added here once that process is complete.

A Few Terms

For readers outside the field

Gaussian Splatting
A way of representing a 3D scene as a large collection of soft, blurry 3D blobs ("Gaussians") instead of a mesh or point cloud — fast to render and well-suited to learning from photographs.
Equirectangular projection (ERP)
The standard way of flattening a 360° spherical image into a rectangle — the same kind of projection used for flat world maps, with the same stretching near the poles.
3DGUT
A Gaussian-splatting method that can render through non-standard camera models (fisheye, 360°, etc.) without needing a separate, custom-built renderer for each one.
Depth prior
An initial estimate of how far away each part of a scene is, usually produced by a pretrained AI model, used to give a reconstruction method a sensible starting point.
